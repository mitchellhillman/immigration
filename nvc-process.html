NVC Process — Approval to Embassy

The National Visa Center stage: what happens between USCIS approval and your embassy interview. Compiled from community reports across multiple embassies. Not legal advice.

Where NVC sits in the flow

USCIS approval (NOA2) → Sent to Dept. of State DOS → NVC case # assigned → Shipped In Transit → Embassy has it Ready → Schedule interview → Welcome letter

The slow, opaque middle stretch. After approval the case is sent to DOS, an NVC case number is assigned, the file is shipped, and the embassy's status moves through In Transit → Ready. Once Ready, you can schedule the interview.

Digital vs physical file: the digital file reaches the embassy fast — In Transit → Ready took ~1 day for Rio. The physical file arrives later; that's what the embassy's "welcome letter" signals. You generally don't need the welcome letter to schedule — many book the interview before it arrives (follow your attorney's advice if you used one).

The "At NVC" → embassy transfer wait

  • Highly variable by embassy and by month. Reported waits ranged from ~3 weeks to 8–9 weeks after the case number was assigned.
  • Status sits at "At NVC" until the transfer happens, then flips toward "In Transit."
  • Several embassies (e.g. Mumbai, Bogotá, Manila) showed slow transfers in mid-2026 — Manila applicants reported waiting since April. A common theory: summer non-immigrant-visa (NIV) demand backing things up. Cause unconfirmed.
  • You're not alone if people who filed after you appear to move first — order isn't strict.

What to prepare while you wait

This stage is mostly waiting for the case number — use it to gather documents:

  • I-134 (Affidavit of Support) — required by essentially all embassies to prove you can support the beneficiary. Whether a joint sponsor is allowed is embassy-dependent.
  • Police record(s) — see the timing caveat below.
  • Financial support proof (if needed) and vaccination documentation.

Time your police record to the interview, not now. They expire — Brazil's are valid 90 days, so applicants get them ~end of June for an August interview. But factor in lead time: the UK's ACRO takes ~3–4 weeks (standard ~20 days, or express), and the UK embassy is reportedly lax on the validity window. Check your own country's validity period and processing time before ordering.

DOS stage & the Public Inquiry (PI)

  • Sent to DOS: on the case tracker (e.g. TMVN) it reads "Case was sent to the Department of State," shown at the top under Case Details. You also get an email on any status change. Reported 1–3 weeks from approval to DOS — not instant.
  • Case number can arrive two ways: via the K-1 FTP letter (often through your attorney) or in response to a PI (Public Inquiry).
  • When to send a PI: most send the first PI ~2 weeks after being sent to DOS / At NVC; average response is ~1 week, so the case number tends to land around the 3-week mark. The official letter quotes up to 60 days, but it's usually faster. By the time one applicant's number came back, the case was already In Transit.
  • You can send another PI if needed. If a reply says it "wasn't received," respond with your NOA2 information to confirm the case reached NVC.
  • Name looks wrong in the email? NVC's reply "To:" line sometimes shows your middle or last name oddly. It's cosmetic — your file is built from the petition / NOA2 data, not your email. If your NOA2 is correct, you're fine.

Being sent to DOS has been notably faster lately — e.g. biometrics → approval in ~8 days, then DOS ~13 days after. Ranges vary wildly though; some wait months for approval.

Decode your NVC case number

The case number encodes when it was created — format AAA + 4-digit year + 3-digit Julian day + 3-digit daily sequence. Example MNL2007547002:

  • MNL — destination consulate (Manila; LND = London, etc.)
  • 2007 — year NVC created the case
  • 547 — subtract 500 → 47 → the 47th day of the year (Julian), i.e. Feb 16
  • 002 — the 2nd case assigned to that consulate that day

Subtract 500 from the Julian-day chunk before reading it, then look it up on a Julian calendar for the year. Lets you pin the exact creation date.

Checking for "Ready" on CEAC

Once the digital file lands, the embassy's CEAC status flips to Ready — often before any other notification. To check the immigrant-visa status by case number:

  • Select Immigrant Visa and enter your NVC case number.
  • If it asks for surname / passport before the case is populated, people get through with NA for surname and NA for passport — or the first ~5 letters of the beneficiary's surname.
  • Match the surname's exact spelling, including spaces (e.g. EL HO with the space) — that alone fixed it for one person.
  • "Your search did not return any data" usually just means it hasn't updated yet. Wait a few hours and retry; it's not a problem.

Embassy appointment slots

  • Embassies control their own slots — NVC doesn't hand them out. Slots open on each post's own cycle.
  • Seoul 🇰🇷: reported roughly every two weeks, on a Wed/Thu/Fri, around 09:00–12:00 KST. Timing isn't guaranteed — updates can lag.
  • Brazil 🇧🇷: reportedly tends to release more slots around the 20th of each month.
  • Check frequently around your post's expected window; slots can fill fast.
  • Seeing just a bare "No slots available" line with no calendar (not even fully-booked dates)? That usually means there genuinely are none right now. If you got a "payment successful" receipt, your fee went through — you don't need a separate embassy confirmation email to be paid; the page simply has nothing to show until slots open.

Tracking the courier shipment (DHL)

Many embassies ship the case file via DHL (Brazil, Paris, and others). Tracking it is hit-or-miss:

  • Open the tracking page on a PC, not a phone — phone often errors.
  • Errors usually mean you're checking too early; try again in a few days.
  • One workaround that worked for some: re-upload the same tracking image from a phone.
  • Tracking failing means nothing. Several people (incl. Brazil) could never get it to work — the case still ships normally. Don't read failure as a problem.

DS-160 form

Once Ready, the next moves are: submit the DS-160, then schedule the visa appointment. Some embassies email you to start the DS-160 before the case even shows Ready.

  • No login. You pick a security question to start. You're given a DS-160 application number — write it down.
  • Save progress at every section. You can fill what you can now and return later. To resume: "Continue your application" + DS-160 number + security question (+ passport).
  • The site can be glitchy and may force a refresh — having the number saved matters.
  • "Did someone assist you in completing this application?" — common reading is that a fiancé(e)/petitioner helping doesn't count; it means a third-party preparer outside the couple. The form doesn't clarify, so interpret conservatively.
  • Helpful walkthrough that flags the easy-to-get-wrong questions: Suraj & Yasmin's DS-160 step-by-step guide.

IMBRA pamphlet

  • You receive the IMBRA pamphlet at the embassy.
  • It's also attached to the embassy welcome letter, and referenced on the appointment confirmation (which tells you to review it before the interview).

DS-160 photo — embassy dependent

  • Whether you upload a photo with the DS-160 varies by embassy.
  • Some require a digital upload (and won't accept a printed photo); fixing a rejected upload is common.
  • Others show "Photo will be taken at ASC" on the DS-160 confirmation page — no upload needed. The embassy is selected early, so the requirement is set from the start.

Interview day — heads-up (varies by embassy)

  • Can the petitioner attend? Embassy-dependent. Rio: no — the petitioner waits outside. Some posts (e.g. DR) allow it; some make it optional. Where optional, many choose to attend given how important it is.
  • 221(g) / social media review: some posts are issuing 221(g) administrative processing for social media checks, which can add ~1–several weeks before the visa is issued. Build this into your flight buffer.
